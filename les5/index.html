Diagnosis of the Situation

The Mushroom Lovers' Concert Attendance Problem

The concert attendance problem can be understood using a logic tree and yes/no decision tree, breaking down the issue into key questions and potential causes.

Logic Tree

Awareness (A)

  • Inadequate promotion or advertising of the concerts.
  • Poor visibility of promotional materials (e.g., posters in low-traffic areas).
  • Lack of online or social media presence.
  • Target audience not clearly defined, leading to ineffective outreach.

Interest (B)

  • People are aware of the concerts but do not find the band appealing.
  • Mismatch between the band's music style and the audience’s preferences.
  • Lack of credibility as a professional band.
  • Competing events scheduled at the same time.

Retention (C)

  • Poor concert experience (e.g., sound quality, performance, or atmosphere).
  • Lack of variety or improvement in the shows over time.
  • Negative word-of-mouth from past attendees.
  • Disorganized event management (e.g., scheduling delays).

Yes/No Tree Questions

  1. Are they aware of the concerts?
    • If NO, focus on improving awareness (A).
    • If YES, move to the next question.
  2. If they are aware, have they ever been to a concert?
    • If NO, focus on increasing interest (B).
    • If YES, move to the next question.
  3. If they have been to a concert, do they attend consistently?
    • If NO, focus on retention strategies (C).
    • If YES, continue nurturing these loyal attendees.

Investigating the Root Causes

  • Conduct Surveys or Interviews: Ask non-attendees why they haven’t attended and gather feedback from past attendees about their experience.
  • Assess Marketing Efforts: Review the effectiveness of promotional campaigns and analyze whether the target audience has been reached.
  • Evaluate the Concert Experience: Check the quality of sound, lighting, and overall performance to see if the events meet or exceed expectations.
  • Benchmark Against Competitors: Study how other local bands attract and retain their audience.

Next Steps

  1. Measure the size of each bucket in the yes/no tree:
    • Who is unaware?
    • Who is aware but uninterested?
    • Who has attended but not returned?
  2. Identify patterns or common themes behind each root cause.
  3. Prioritize addressing the most significant issues first:
    • Awareness campaigns (social media, posters, partnerships).
    • Enhancing the value of the concert experience.
    • Building credibility as a band through collaborations or community engagement.

Yes/No Tree for Oversleeping

  1. Did the alarm go off?
    • YES: Proceed to Q2.
    • NO:
      • The alarm wasn’t set.
      • The alarm was set for the wrong time.
      • The device (clock/phone) ran out of battery or power.
  2. Did you hear the alarm?
    • YES: Proceed to Q3.
    • NO:
      • The alarm volume was too low.
      • The alarm tone wasn’t loud/noticeable enough.
      • You were in a deep sleep cycle.
  3. Did you wake up but go back to sleep?
    • YES:
      • You were too tired from lack of sleep.
      • You thought you had more time and snoozed the alarm.
      • You lacked motivation to get out of bed.
    • NO:
      • External distractions or unknown reasons kept you from waking up.
